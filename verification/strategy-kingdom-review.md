## What this game is (as discovered)

A turn-based kingdom sim. Pick a ruler profile (Player One / Player Two / Guest — fully isolated saves), run "reigns" of 12–24 seasons. Assign workers to Farms/Lumber Camps/Quarries/Markets/Libraries, build on a 12-plot grid, answer advisor event cards, research a 10-node discovery tree with prerequisites. Reigns end with a written history of your run, a transparent score formula, and a wax-seal rank (Steward → Reeve → Magistrate → Monarch). Meta layer: 3 escalating reigns, 7 unlockable banners (equippable, visible in play), 6 badges. Sound toggle plus read-aloud speaker buttons on every text panel for the early reader.

## Pillar 1 — Competence, not patronized: 5/5

This game never once talks down. The register is "you are the ruler, here is your counsel": "Who is ruling today?", "An advisor brings a choice. Read both options. The numbers show exactly what happens." There is zero hollow praise in the entire game — no "Great job, superstar!", no confetti copy. Feedback is consequence stated plainly: "A surplus grew your kingdom by one person." Even failure is handled with dignity I rarely see in adult games: I missed the tutorial goal (12/14 people) and got a written chronicle ending "The reign has ended. The kingdom stands, and your work is recorded," then a Steward seal — the lowest rank, stamped without apology or shame. Error states are equally respectful: "No free hands. Take a worker off somewhere else." It uses real strategy-game grammar (per-turn deltas, build costs, tech prerequisites) and trusts kids to learn it. The babyish-anywhere scan came back empty.

## Pillar 2 — Depth & progression: 4/5

There is a real arc. The Tutorial Reign teaches the loop with an embedded Counsel coach; the Growth Reign removes the coach entirely and replaces that panel with the research tree — training wheels off, new system on, bigger goal (25 people/20 seasons). Prosperity Reign raises it to a three-condition goal (28 people + 80 gold + 6 discoveries) and is gated behind finishing Growth. Mastery accumulates visibly: per-reign best scores ("Best: Reeve · 29 pts" on the 6-year-old's profile), the four-rank seal ladder, banners with concrete unlock conditions that actually equip and fly over your kingdom, badges for playstyle feats (surplus every turn; 100 gold; all 10 discoveries). Build order carries real strategic weight — I built a Library before a Quarry and crippled an entire 20-season run, a genuine strategy-game consequence. Session 5 will not feel like session 1.

Why not 5: the event deck shows its bottom quickly (~6 card types in 20+ turns, mostly the same +people/−food vs +gold trade reskinned); a stalled kingdom can mean many seasons of literal nothing-to-do (see punch item 2); and the endgame state machine frays (overtime says "season 13 of 12," a card reads "No reign yet" beside an "In progress" button), which dulls the payoff of the arc.

## Pillar 3 — Learning emerges from play: 5/5

This is the best math-embedding I've seen in a kids' game. The multiplication is not a gate you answer — it is the lever you pull. Tapping a building opens a stepper that reads `3 × 3 = 9 food`, and adding a worker rewrites the equation live while the top resource bar flips from red `-6/turn` to green `+3/turn`. Different buildings run different tables (×1 library, ×2 lumber/quarry/market, ×3 farm), and discoveries literally increment the multiplier — after Irrigation, the food formula visibly becomes `3 × 4 = 12`. Wanting bigger numbers makes a kid want research; that is the whole loop. Every resource chip opens an itemized income statement ("Farm: 3 × 3 = 9 food / 7 people eat 7 / +2 surplus"), so surplus/deficit reasoning, comparison, and even division (score: "Gold ÷ 5") are all load-bearing play, not homework. Event cards are arithmetic word problems with stakes ("Take them in: +2 people, −8 food" — can my larder carry 11 eaters on 9 food?), and the game grades you with consequences, never a red X. Nothing here smells like a quiz.

## Pillar 4 — Kid-would-choose-it: 3/5

**The first two minutes:** tap your name on a handsome dark title screen → a throne-room hub that is, frankly, a lot of words → "Begin reign" → and then the good part: a board that is already in trouble (FOOD 8, −6/turn, in red), one clear instruction from your Counsel, and your first worker assignment makes the equation grow and the red number turn green. First build follows a turn later. For the 8-year-old, this lands — agency, legible numbers, a goal chip ticking toward 14. For the 6-year-old, the first screen is a wall of muted slate and serif text; the read-aloud buttons rescue it only if they find them, and their reward for doing the right thing is... a number changing. No one cheers, nothing moves. The honest answer on the home-screen duel: this wins the 8-year-old who likes systems (and the seal/banner/rank loop is a real "one more reign" hook), and it loses the 6-year-old's unsupervised first tap to the candy-colored ad game unless a parent sits through reign one with them. The kingdom never visibly lives — no villagers walking, no smoke, no festival animation when you literally hold a festival. The bones (short bounded sessions, tap-tap-end-turn cadence, story-choice event cards) are exactly right; it needs juice, not sugar.

## Babyish-anywhere scan

**Clean.** No babyish copy, no hollow praise, no talking-down found in any panel, button, event card, formula, or end screen. The art is austere rather than cute — the opposite failure mode, and the right one to have. The only "soft" mechanic is starvation: at 0 food nobody dies or leaves, growth just stalls with a red "need 2 more food." Defensible for the age band, but the consequence is so quiet a kid may not register they're failing (a one-line Counsel nudge exists; the people could also look visibly hungry).

## Strongest single moment

Opening the Farm sheet and watching `0 × 3 = 0` become `3 × 3 = 9` while the resource bar's red `−6/turn` flips to green `+3/turn` in real time — cause, computation, and consequence in one gesture. That moment IS the game's thesis, it happens in the first 60 seconds, and it's genuinely satisfying. (Runner-up: the wax seal stamping a rank onto a written history of your specific reign.)

## Top-5 punch list (most impactful first)

1. **Stop the buttons lying:** when housing is full, a "+2 people, −6 food" event still charges the 6 food and silently delivers zero people (verified twice: festival and travelers while at cap) — disable the option with "No room — build a House first," scale the cost to people actually housed, or show the clamp on the button; this is the one place the game breaks its own "every number shown" covenant.
2. **Kill the stone softlock:** the Quarry costs 2 stone, so spending your starting 4 stone on anything else (e.g., a Library) leaves zero stone income and no way to build houses/markets/quarries until the random Master Mason card appears — mine took 7 dead seasons to arrive; make the first Quarry cost wood only, or guarantee a stone-rescue card within 2 seasons of detecting the lock.
3. **Add juice for the 6-year-old at the three payoff moments** — a new villager visibly walks in when population grows, buildings pop with a flourish when construction finishes, and the seal stamps with weight and sound at reign's end — keeping the prose exactly as dry as it is now.
4. **Tell one consistent completion story:** the end screen says "New reign unlocked" while First Light ("Complete the Tutorial Reign") stays unearned and the pennant stays locked, overtime renders as "season 13 of 12," and the Growth card shows "No reign yet" beside "In progress" — pick one model (e.g., "Reign recorded — the goal stands. Keep ruling or reign again") and make every surface agree.
5. **Deepen the event deck:** ~6 card archetypes recycle the same people-vs-gold trade; add stone/wood crises, delayed-consequence cards that pay off three seasons later, and cards that reference the player's actual kingdom ("Your second farm has drawn settlers") so season 18 doesn't feel like season 4.

## Bugs & wobbles noticed (beyond the punch list)

- Banked research over the cost displays as "Studying Wheelbarrow: 6 of 5 research" instead of completing immediately or clamping the display.
- Post-summary "overtime" play is discarded by a mid-overtime reload (the scored result survives; the season-13 continuation does not).
- Reset-kingdom uses a native browser `confirm()` — functional, but jarringly out-of-world on what is otherwise a fully themed UI (and on an iPad PWA it will look like a system error).
- Worker-sheet backdrop swallows taps on the board behind it (correct modal behavior, but a tap-happy kid will tap a building twice and feel ignored — a subtle shake or auto-switch to the tapped building would feel better).
- Console is clean across the entire session (zero errors; one deprecated `apple-mobile-web-app-capable` meta warning).

## Verdict

**POLISH.** Pillars 1 and 3 are as good as this genre gets for kids — a dignified strategy game where arithmetic is the steering wheel. Fix the two trust-breakers first: the cap-clamped event rewards that charge full price for nothing (item 1) and the stone softlock that can becalm a 6-year-old for seven turns (item 2). Add payoff juice (item 3) and this beats the ad-funded slop on its own terms: the 8-year-old will choose it tonight; the 6-year-old will choose it once something on screen celebrates with them.
