## 1. Competence, not patronized — 5/5

This is the game's spine and it never cracks. The fiction is played completely straight: CONFIDENTIAL stamps, manila case files, a typewriter-set ID card, ranks from Cadet to Chief. The copy treats the player like staff, not like a toddler: "Your first case is on the desk. Open it when you're ready." The cancel button in the accusation dialog says "Not yet." A briefing reminds you "Nobody is in trouble until the evidence says so." The badge section opens with the best line in the game: "They're earned by how you work a case, not how fast." Failure feedback is the real test and it passes beautifully — cite the wrong clue and you get logic, not consolation: "Mateo matches that detail, so it doesn't clear them." Accuse the wrong kid and you get method, not shame: "The evidence doesn't point to Sofia. Check who each clue rules out." Zero exclamation-point praise, zero mascots, zero baby-talk anywhere I looked.

## 2. Depth & progression — 3/5

The progression *skeleton* is genuinely excellent: 30 cases across three tiers, six ranks, an itemized XP ledger after every case, eight process-based commendations, ID-card cosmetic unlocks at each rank, and per-kid desks so mastery is visibly *theirs*. Crucially, XP rewards process quality, not just completion — my sloppy Case 1 (hint + wrong accusation) paid +125 while a clean methodical Case 2 paid +245 (first-try +50, hint-free +40, methodical +30), and Tier II raises base pay to +150. That is honest, legible incentive design. The problem is the *content* under the skeleton: through 12 cases the puzzle verb never changes — match two attribute clues against three (then four) suspects' trait icons. Clue sentences recycle verbatim ("The culprit was seen earlier walking a dog" appears in Cases 1, 3, and 10), titles repeat brazenly (Cases 3 and 4 are *both* "The Case of the Borrowed Umbrella," back to back; 6 and 8 share "Vanishing Trophy"; 11 and 12 reuse earlier titles), and suspect names recycle with shifting hair colors (Mateo is brown-haired, then blond, then red). Escalation so far is one more suspect and an alibi-type clue — quantity, not new reasoning. Session 5 will feel like session 1 with an extra column unless the tiers diversify the *kind* of thinking.

## 3. Learning emerges from play — 4/5

No quiz-ware anywhere. The learning lives entirely inside the game's verbs: read a clue (real reading practice for a 6-year-old, with a speaker button on literally every piece of text), compare it against suspect traits, and — the standout mechanic — clear a suspect by citing *which specific clue* exonerates them. That's evidence-based argumentation, not multiple choice. The accusation itself demands a claim plus two supporting clues. The post-case "How you closed it" debrief replays the full deduction chain in plain language ("The cap clue meant the culprit wore a cap... That cleared Esme and Leo. Zoe is the only one left"), which is free metacognition. The hint ladder teaches method before answers ("Look at the hair clues. One of them rules someone out"), escalates to a worked inference, then caps itself ("Inference shown" — the button disables; mashing gets you nothing more). One point withheld: the reasoning is single-step elimination throughout, and the accuse dialog shows all clue texts plus all suspect traits in one place, so a clever kid can compress the whole case into ten seconds of icon-matching — the game even tips +50/Quick Study for it. The careful path out-earns the shortcut by only 30 XP.

## 4. Kid-would-choose-it — 3/5

The first two minutes are honestly strong. Tap your own name on a desk that belongs to *you* → moody detective office, your ID card, a sticky note saying your first case is waiting → open the file, CONFIDENTIAL stamp, two picture clues, three suspects → name the culprit → a big red **CASE CLOSED** stamp slams over the screen, your reasoning is read back to you, an XP ledger counts up, a badge lands. First payoff arrives inside three minutes and it feels *earned*. Identity + fast competence is a real hook, and two siblings with separate desks and visible ranks will race each other. So it wins the tap this week. The risk is next week: against a freemium iPad game the presentation is static — suspects are rows of small icons with no faces (an 8-year-old will ask what they look like), there's no motion outside the stamp, and by Case 4–6 a kid will personally hit the seams: the same music-room-trophy madlib, two cases in a row with the identical title, and clues about someone "walking a bird." The 8-year-old will clear Tier 1 in one sitting and start noticing the game is shuffling, not authoring. The 6-year-old's needs are well covered (read-aloud everywhere, three text sizes), which buys goodwill — but retention past session 3–4 depends on variety the content layer doesn't have yet.

## Babyish-anywhere scan

**Clean.** Copy, visuals, and feedback moments all hold the adult-noir register. No hollow praise, no celebration confetti, no talking down — wrong answers are answered with reasoning. The only tonal wobbles are template artifacts, which read as *sloppy*, not babyish: "The culprit was seen earlier walking a bird" (and "walking a cat"), one briefing starting lowercase ("the hall pass vanished from the park..."), and the reset confirmation's odd "Type nothing — just confirm." Settings deserve credit: sound described in-fiction ("Stamps, chimes, page sounds"), and the destructive reset is guarded by a confirm with "Keep my progress" as the safe option.

## Strongest single moment

The first **CASE CLOSED** stamp slamming down with "Mateo did it." — followed immediately by "How you closed it," where the game replays *your* logic back to you like a senior detective initialing your report. The pair turns a kid's guess into a documented deduction; it's the moment the fantasy ("I'm a real detective") and the pedagogy (here's the inference chain) fuse. Runner-up: the calm forensic correction when you cite a bad clue — "Mateo matches that detail, so it doesn't clear them" — failure handled as information.

## Top-5 punch list (most impactful first)

1. **Make each case one coherent, unique story seed** — title, briefing, location, and stolen object must agree and never repeat: today Cases 3 and 4 are both "The Case of the Borrowed Umbrella" back to back, and "Untied Shoelaces" briefs about a trophy in the music room while its clue says the gym.
2. **Change the reasoning, not just the roster, per tier** — Tier II should introduce decoy clues a kid must reject (the Red-Herring Hunter badge already promises this) and Tier III two-step inferences, so session 5 plays differently than session 1.
3. **Fix the clue/briefing copy templates** — "seen earlier walking a bird/cat" → pet-appropriate phrasing, capitalize sentence starts, and write 3–4 alternate phrasings per attribute so identical sentences stop recurring across cases.
4. **Give suspects simple portrait faces** (flat vector busts above the trait icons) so clearing someone feels like exonerating a person rather than deleting a row of icons.
5. **Persist mid-case state across reloads** — cleared suspects and hint state currently reset to zero on refresh (XP/closures survive); iPad Safari tab-eviction will eat a kid's half-worked case.

Honorable mention: widen the XP gap between the methodical path and the straight-to-accuse shortcut (currently 245 vs 215 + a Quick Study badge for the shortcut) so the game's stated values and its payouts fully agree.

## Verdict: POLISH

Nothing structural is broken — the loop is sound, the tone is exactly right, the progression skeleton is better than most paid kids' apps, and the learning is genuinely inside the verbs. But the case-content layer is a thin madlib whose seams (duplicate titles, recycled verbatim clues, nonsense pet phrasing) are visible to an 8-year-old by the fourth case. Ship after punch items 1–3; items 4–5 make it durable.

**Technical note:** Zero console errors across the full session; the single warning is a deprecated `apple-mobile-web-app-capable` meta tag (the iPad home-screen intent is otherwise in place). Reload, profile isolation (the 6-year-old's 155 XP desk untouched by Guest's run), hint-mash cap, double-click mash, and the reset flow all behaved correctly.
