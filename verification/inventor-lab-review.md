## 1. Competence, not patronized — 5/5

This is the best-voiced kids' game copy I have reviewed. There is not one exclamation point in the entire game. Profiles are "Who's at the bench?"; mission cards carry REV counters like real drawings; star criteria are written as specs ("≤ 2 parts, ≤ 20 cost"); failures are logged, not mourned ("TEST #2 · LOGGED — Off the edge. The path needs a catch there."); and the win line after a long grind is the game's thesis in six words: "Solved in 7 tests. That is the job." A clean first-try win gets a drier "It holds. Mission approved." — the game modulates its respect. Badges reward the process itself ("Test Pilot — Run 10 tests. Engineers test things."). Babyish-anywhere scan came back empty: no mascot hand-holding, no hollow praise, no "Oopsie," no candy palette — the only cuteness is a tiny face on the payload ball, which reads as a crew member, not a babysitter. The read-aloud button on every brief (verified: real speech synthesis) serves the early reader without dumbing the text down. Exemplary.

## 2. Depth & progression — 3/5

There is a real arc: three mission families (Bridge / Ball Run / Launch) that genuinely play differently, a parts ecosystem that grows (Plank → Ramp wedge → Bouncer pad), star targets that turn "solved" into "solved efficiently," pens that unlock at star totals (14/22/30/36), category badges, a lifetime test counter, and REV counts that persist on mission cards as visible scar tissue. Session 5 will absolutely feel different from session 1 — by Mission 11 you are tuning a two-stage drop-and-fling contraption, nothing like dropping the first plank. But the ceiling is close: 12 missions, three part types observed, all missions open from the start (no gating — which I count as respect, but it also means no paced reveal), and critically **no sandbox/free-build mode** — "My Inventions" is a trophy shelf of saved winning builds, not a workshop. Difficulty is uneven: Mission 6 solves on the first tap; Mission 11 demands ~20px trajectory precision out of 23px snap rows and 15° rotation steps; and Mission 10 may be broken outright (see punch list — after systematic analysis I could find no path to the crate with the parts given, and this device's save shows it as the only never-attempted-successfully mission while later missions in the same family are approved). An invisible no-build zone in M10 blocks the obvious bridge solve with only a subtle amber ghost tint as explanation.

## 3. Learning emerges from play — 4/5

The strongest design idea here: the curriculum IS the physics, and the teaching instrument is the **trace** — a dotted line showing exactly where the payload went, every test. Fail diagnoses name the failure mode (off the edge / stopped short / did not clear / settled) and hint with a question ("More ramp?"), never an answer. There is no quiz, no fact card, no "did you know" anywhere. It worked on me, measurably: across Missions 1-2 the trace taught me that flat roads bleed speed, landings kill momentum, and ramps must meet edges flush — pure scientific method (baseline test, observe, hypothesize, change one thing, retest), wearing a game's clothes. The point deducted is for the physics tuning undermining the lesson: rolling friction is so savage that a ball visibly **crawls down a 15° ramp and drops off the end dead-vertically** (screenshot: review-il-failure-trace.png shows the related stopped-at-the-lip case). That contradicts every playground intuition a 6-year-old owns. The trace shows WHERE the ball went but never WHY it slowed; to a kid, some stops will read as the game cheating rather than physics happening.

## 4. Kid-would-choose-it — 3/5

The first two minutes, narrated honestly: tap your own name on a dark blueprint screen (instant ownership — Netflix-style benches, stars saved per engineer); land on a clean mission board with zero popups, zero ads, zero currencies, zero account nags — the contrast with the freemium incumbents is itself striking; tap First Span; a three-sentence brief (tap the speaker and it reads itself to the early reader); tap Plank, tap the canvas — a part snaps in, inked in your equipped pen color; hit TEST — and within two seconds either a green APPROVED stamp slams diagonally across your blueprint (the best feedback moment in the game, and a real dopamine spike) or the ball stops at the very lip of the gap with a dotted trace daring you to fix it. When that loop runs clean, it beats slop. What costs it the tap on a bad day: stall runs — when the ball creeps and settles, the test takes a silent 8-13 seconds (a clean win resolves in ~2s) with no skip and no sound of progress, which is where a 6-year-old taps the screen, gets nothing, and leaves; placed parts **cannot be dragged to reposition** (mouse-verified; rotate/delete only), so every adjustment is delete-and-replace; and rejected placements fail silently except for an amber ghost tint. The 8-year-old plausibly chooses this over an ad-funded runner on most days — the stamp, their own bench, and the next pen are real pulls. The 6-year-old gets two great missions and then needs a co-pilot. With items 1-3 below fixed, this is a 4, maybe higher.

## Strongest single moment

The APPROVED stamp (review-il-approved-stamp.png): after seven logged failures, a green engineering stamp slams over your drawing reading "MISSION 01 · REV 8," the targets check off like a spec sheet, and the game says "Solved in 7 tests. That is the job." It converts a failure count into professional pride — the exact inversion of how every other kids' game treats being wrong, and the single best reason this game deserves to exist.

## Editor abuse report

- **Solid:** TEST/RETEST fully debounced (8 rapid clicks = 1 run); parts and palette hard-locked during runs; deleting refunds budget; overlap placement blocked with a cyan-to-amber ghost tint; zero console errors across the entire abusive session; engine never crashed.
- **Broke:** after ~15 minutes of aggressive fiddling in Mission 10 I produced two planks stacked at identical coordinates that could not be selected or deleted, and RESET did not clear them or restore the budget — only leaving to the bench and re-entering recovered. Stray taps after a silent placement rejection drop parts in random places (the armed palette lingers).
- **Reload mid-build:** returns to the profile picker; stars/REVs/badges persist per profile (verified the 6-year-old's 16/36 vs the 8-year-old's isolated), but the in-progress build is lost — on an iPad that reloads Safari tabs aggressively, kids will lose work.
- **Misc:** NEXT on the win screen goes to the bench, not the next mission; badges earn silently (no toast — the 8-year-old earned two and would never know).

## Top-5 punch list (most impactful first)

1. **Make placed parts draggable** (and verify touch-drag on the actual iPad) — delete-and-replace as the only way to nudge a part is the single biggest friction for small hands, and clicking a part currently micro-shifts it ~6px as a bonus confusion.
2. **Retune rolling friction** so balls visibly accelerate downhill and never crawl off a 15° ramp into a dead vertical drop — the current model contradicts kid intuition and makes "stopped short" feel arbitrary instead of instructive.
3. **End stalled tests early** — if payload speed is ~0 for 1.5s, call the result immediately (or add a skip/2x button): the 8-13 second silent creep is where the 6-year-old's attention dies.
4. **Audit Mission 10's solvability** with only Plank+Ramp given the invisible no-build zone — systematic analysis found no path to the crate, and this save's history is consistent with a kid bouncing off it; if it needs the Bouncer, grant the Bouncer (and render no-build zones visibly, e.g., hatched).
5. **Celebrate at the moment of earning** — badge toast on the win screen, a brief shake plus one-line reason on rejected placements, and send NEXT to the next mission, not the bench.

## Verdict

**POLISH.** No pillar is structurally broken — the voice, the test-trace-iterate loop, and the stamp are genuinely better than anything on the kids' current home screen. Items 1-3 are tuning and juice, not rework. Fix those, sanity-check Mission 10, and put it on the iPad.

## Final re-verdict (2026-06-12)
- Punch item verification:
  - Drag-to-move + select micro-shift → **resolved.** Tap is select-only: the plank sat at exactly (417.5, 245.9) through select/deselect/reselect cycles, zero movement, with a −15°/+15°/del control cluster appearing above it. Mouse-drag moves placed parts freely (417→550 px in one gesture) and lands on the snap grid (vertical steps measured at clean row increments). Delete-and-replace is dead as the only nudge path.
  - Sluggish rolling physics → **resolved.** Balls now behave like playground balls: my Mission 2 win required the ball to fly a 46 px gap off the END of a flat plank on carried momentum; in Mission 3 my deliberate V-shaped stall trap backfired because the ball crested the uphill plank and won the mission; Mission 10's hammer-ball launched off a −15° tilt and knocked the crate clean into the basin. No dead-vertical crawl-offs observed in ~15 tests.
  - Silent 8–13 s stalls → **resolved.** An engineered pocket-trap settle was called in 3.1 s with a named diagnosis ("Everything settled. The ball stopped short."), and a "TAP TO SKIP" pill is on screen during every run (from ~250 ms) and actually works — tapped at 700 ms, result logged at 836 ms. One boundary case: Mission 10's empty-build run took ~12 s because the ball was genuinely still rattling in the pit (not a frozen stall), but the skip was visible the entire time, so dead air is now opt-out.
  - Mission 10 unsolvable / invisible no-build zone → **resolved.** The level is redesigned ("The ball is your hammer... knock the crate off its pedestal") and I solved it blind in 5 tests within the ≤1-part star target: end-wall fail → flush-but-low fail ("stopped short") → single plank tilted −15° → crate knocked into the basin. Every fail produced a legible physical diagnosis; no invisible rejection anywhere I built. Placement rejections now show an amber dashed box with an X and the literal label "Parts overlap" — exactly the visible cue asked for.
  - Badge earns invisible → **resolved.** Completing the bridge family popped "COMMENDATION · BRIDGE BUILDER" as a ribbon on the APPROVED win screen at the moment of earning, persisting several seconds. Bonus beyond the claim: NEXT now goes to the next mission, not the bench.
  - New since review — stacked-duplicate corruption → **fixed** (placing a part at identical coordinates is rejected by the overlap guard; could not reproduce stuck parts; budget stayed arithmetic-consistent through abuse and RESET restored a clean snapshot). Impact dust → **verified** (particle burst at the landing point, fading over ~400 ms, caught on clipped frames). Near-miss hardening → **verified by honest play**: won M2 with a road ending 46 px short, M10 with a plank one snap-row off flush and 5 px off the lip, M5 first-try with approximate two-plank staging across the pillar. Solved 5 missions this session (M2, M3, M4, M5, M10 — 15 tests logged), all 3-starred.
- Regressions noticed: none. Reload still returns to the profile picker with the in-progress build lost (pre-existing, not claimed; progress/stars/badges persisted correctly — 18/36 verified post-reload). Rotate/del controls are 25 px circles — small for a 6-year-old finger, though drag now carries the main interaction load. Console: zero app errors across the whole session (same deprecated-meta warning only).
- FINAL VERDICT: **SHIP** — Every punch item is resolved the way the original review asked, and the three quieter claims (overlap guard, dust, near-miss tolerance) all held up under deliberate abuse and honest imperfect building. The fixes compound: drag makes iteration cheap, livelier physics makes the trace trustworthy, fast settles plus skip keep failure under three seconds, the reject cue turns the editor's one mute moment into copy ("Parts overlap") that even reads in-register, and Mission 10 went from a wall to the best lesson in the Launch family. Pillar changes: Learning emerges from play 4 → **5** (the single deduction — friction contradicting kid intuition — is gone; the trace now shows arcs a 6-year-old will believe), Kid-would-choose-it 3 → **4** (the review predicted exactly this if items 1–3 were fixed; stall dead-air, the biggest session-killer for the 6-year-old, is now skippable), Competence 5 (unchanged — "COMMENDATION" is perfect), Depth & progression holds at 3 (still 12 missions, still no sandbox, and I did not re-verify M11's precision demands — that is the next content pass, not a ship blocker). Put it on the iPad.
